Overlays Design Pattern Research

In her article Best Practices for Modals / Overlays / Dialog Windows, Naema Baskanderi details how coders can use overlays in a manner that is both user-friendly and effective.

I found the advice that Baskanderi offers very useful as it largely centers around usability. She asserts that a title to an overlay should be descriptive, and if a button is clicked, the title should be the button label, word for word. Overlays shouldn't come out of nowhere, but rather they should be prompted by user interaction. It should be easy for a user to exit an overlay–with a clear exit button and the ability to use an exit key (like esc). Overlays are the most effective when the background parent page is shadowed or blurred.

Baskanderi notes that users will automatically close pop-up windows and overlays. Even so, an effective overlay can increase usability and engagement within a website, and overlays can save space on a webpage. As a relatively new coder, I feel like it's important to know what to avoid/focus on when using new techniques, so I found this article to be very informative and helpful!

Form Design Research

In order to research form design, I read an article called Best Practices for Form Design by Salim Ansari. In Ansari's article, he details some of the ways in which designers can create forms that are effective and easy to use. I found some of his tips to be incredibly helpful and unintuitive.

One example is that he expresses the issues associated with using inline labels and placeholder text: this text disappears when users start typing. It’s better to have discrete “helper” text nearby that will stay put and make the process of filling out a form easier.

Ansari said something in his article that resonates with me: “As product designers, it is our responsibility to ensure that users of all abilities should be able to access what we create.” As a designer, I try to remain vigilant in learning about ways in which the designs I create are ensuring accessibility. People fill out forms for a variety of important reasons, so designers need to make sure that these forms are readable, easy to navigate, and organized in a way that encourages and aids users in filling them out.

I’m always interested in learning about user experiences with designs. Perhaps the valuable lesson I have learned as a designer thus far is that accessibility issues are not all intuitive. We need to do research and ask questions in order to make designs that function properly for everybody who needs to access them!

An example of an online form that I find to be really effective is Omanda Health's contact form. The webpage itself is simple and easy to nagivate. There is an initial bar that asks what users need help with, and subsequent forms that follow. They have inline text in the form boxes, but they also include clear outside text that gives users the easiest possible user experience.
